HIERARCHICAL REASONING PIPELINE (FOLLOW IN ORDER)
Stage 0: Input sanity and uncertainty
- Determine seizure type and whether classification is confident vs mixed/uncertain.
- If seizure type is uncertain or mixed, prefer broad-spectrum options compatible with multiple seizure types and be cautious about drugs known to worsen generalized seizure types.

Stage 1: Seizure-type compatibility shortlist (select AND reject)
- Actively select the most relevant candidate drugs for the seizure type (do not only eliminate).
- Explicitly reject or strongly deprioritize drugs that are mismatched or known to worsen that seizure type.

Stage 2: Hard safety and special-population modifiers (strong penalties)
Apply strong penalties or near-exclusion when applicable:
- Pregnancy or female of childbearing potential: strongly penalize VPA; minimize polytherapy.
- Hepatic impairment or hepatic failure risk: strongly penalize VPA.
- Low albumin states (renal/hepatic disease, pregnancy, malnutrition): be cautious with highly protein-bound drugs (PHT, VPA) since free levels can change.

Stage 3: Practicality, Cost, and The LMIC Baseline
- In this resource-limited Uganda setting, older/cheaper generation drugs (VPA, CBZ, PB) are the foundational baseline of care. 
- You MUST anchor your initial choices to VPA (for generalized/mixed) or CBZ (for focal) as the default first-line agents.
- You should ONLY pivot to newer, more expensive agents (LEV, LTG, TPM) if there is a strict, documented contraindication to the older drugs (e.g., female of childbearing age requiring VPA avoidance, or severe interaction risk). Do not default to LEV/LTG just because they have "cleaner" profiles in a vacuum.
- Note: PB is highly accessible and used often here despite its sedation. Do not penalize it to 0.0 unless strictly contraindicated.

Stage 4: Side-effect alignment and comorbidity tie-breakers (soft optimization)
- Use cognition vulnerability, mood/behavior risk, weight concerns, and other risks to choose among remaining reasonable options.
- Grounded examples:
  - LEV: behavioral disturbance risk can matter.
  - TPM: cognitive/language issues; weight loss; metabolic acidosis; nephrolithiasis.
  - VPA: weight gain and metabolic issues; hyperammonemia risk (especially relevant with certain contexts); pancreatitis risk is noted.
  - PB: sedating.

Stage 5: Monotherapy first, polytherapy logic if needed
- Prefer one strong option if reasonable.
- If adding an adjunct, prefer a different mechanism where possible.
- Avoid combining multiple sodium-channel dominant drugs (CBZ, PHT, LTG to varying degrees) unless clearly justified.
- If considering VPA + LTG synergy, apply pregnancy and hepatic constraints first.

SEIZURE-TYPE COMPATIBILITY GUIDANCE FOR THESE 10 DRUGS
Use as candidate-pool guidance, not a rigid lookup table.

Focal seizures
- Common first options: CBZ, LTG.
- Alternatives if first options unsuitable: LEV, VPA.
- Adjunct options can include CLB.
- PHT and PB can be considered but weigh adverse effects and interaction burden.

Generalized tonic-clonic seizures (GTCS)
- Strong efficacy option: VPA.
- If VPA unsuitable (especially female of childbearing potential): consider LTG, LEV, TPM.
- CLB can be used as adjunct.
- Caution: LTG can aggravate myoclonus (relevant if myoclonic features coexist).

Absence seizures
- Strong options: ESM or VPA.
- Alternative option: LTG.
- If absence plus GTCS: prefer VPA over ESM.
In high-confidence absence seizure contexts, strongly avoid CBZ and PHT due to seizure worsening risk.

Myoclonic seizures
- Strong option: VPA.
- Alternatives: LEV, TPM.
- Avoid/worsening: CBZ and PHT can worsen myoclonic seizures.
- Caution: LTG can aggravate myoclonus in some contexts.

Tonic or atonic seizures
- Tonic: PHT and LTG are described as effective.
- Atonic: VPA is described as drug of choice for atonic contexts, but may be less effective for tonic control.
- Benzodiazepines (CLB, CZP) may be used as adjuncts depending on context.

BROAD VS NARROW SPECTRUM HEURISTIC (WHEN TYPE UNCERTAIN)
- Broad-spectrum among these 10: LEV, LTG, TPM, VPA, CZP, CLB.
- Narrower focal-leaning among these 10: CBZ, PHT.
- PB is widely used but has sedation and interaction burden.

DRUG-SPECIFIC SAFETY NOTES
CBZ
- Focal-first option; enzyme inducer; interaction burden; risks include rash and hyponatremia (and rare severe reactions noted in clinical references).

CLB, CZP
- Often used as adjuncts across seizure types in the provided sources.

ESM
- Absence-first option; deprioritize if absence plus GTCS (then VPA preferred).

LTG
- Focal and generalized option; caution for myoclonus aggravation; requires slow titration due to serious rash risk. If rapid initiation is needed, consider options that can be started quickly such as LEV, while still matching seizure type and patient factors.

LEV
- Broad-spectrum; can be used without prolonged titration compared to LTG; watch behavioral effects.

PB
- Sedating; enzyme inducer; interaction burden.

PHT
- Enzyme inducer; interaction with hormonal contraception; protein-binding considerations; can worsen myoclonic seizures; chronic adverse effects and bone health concerns are noted in clinical references.

TPM
- Broad-spectrum; cognitive/language effects; weight loss; metabolic acidosis; kidney stone risk; TPM + VPA can increase hyperammonemia risk, so use caution if considering this combination.

VPA
- Strong efficacy across GTCS, absence with GTCS, myoclonic, and atonic contexts.
- Strong caution: avoid in pregnancy and minimize in women of childbearing potential due to teratogenicity and neurodevelopmental risks.
- Hepatic risk: avoid in hepatic failure; hepatotoxicity concerns.
- Metabolic effects: weight gain and metabolic issues.
- Hyperammonemia risk (avoid in urea cycle disorder contexts); pancreatitis risk noted.
- Protein-binding considerations in low albumin states.
